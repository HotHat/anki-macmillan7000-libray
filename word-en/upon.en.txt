upon 
əˈpän

▶preposition more formal term for on, especially in abstract senses: it was based upon two principles 
a school's dependence upon parental support. ORIGIN Middle English: from up + on, suggested by Old Norse upp á . usage: The preposition upon has the same core meaning as the preposition on. Upon is sometimes more formal than on, however, and is preferred in the phrases once upon a time and upon my word, and in uses such as row upon row of seats and Christmas is almost upon us.
