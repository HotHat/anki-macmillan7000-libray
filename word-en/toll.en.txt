toll
1 
tōl

▶noun
1 a charge payable for permission to use a particular bridge or road: turnpike tolls 
[as modifier] : a toll bridge. 
• North American a charge for a long-distance telephone call.
2 [in singular] the number of deaths, casualties, or injuries arising from particular circumstances, such as a natural disaster, conflict, or accident: the toll of dead and injured mounted. 
• the cost or damage resulting from something: the environmental toll of the policy has been high. 
▶verb [with object] (usually as noun tolling) charge a toll for the use of (a bridge or road): the report advocates expressway tolling. 
PHRASES
take its toll (or take a heavy toll) have an adverse effect, especially so as to cause damage, suffering, or death: years of pumping iron have taken their toll on his body.ORIGIN Old English (denoting a charge, tax, or duty), from medieval Latin toloneum, alteration of late Latin teloneum, from Greek telōnion ‘tollhouse,’ from telos ‘tax.’ Sense
2 of the noun (late 19th century) arose from the notion of paying a toll or tribute in human lives (to an adversary or to death).
