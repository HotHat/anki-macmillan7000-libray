metaphor 
ˈmedəˌfôr ˈmedəˌfər

▶noun a figure of speech in which a word or phrase is applied to an object or action to which it is not literally applicable: “I had fallen through a trapdoor of depression,” said Mark, who was fond of theatrical metaphors 
her poetry depends on suggestion and metaphor. 
• a thing regarded as representative or symbolic of something else, especially something abstract: the amounts of money being lost by the company were enough to make it a metaphor for an industry that was teetering. ORIGIN late 15th century: from French métaphore, via Latin from Greek metaphora, from metapherein ‘to transfer.’
