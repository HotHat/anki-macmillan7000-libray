estate 
iˈstāt

▶noun
1 an extensive area of land in the country, usually with a large house, owned by one person, family, or organization. 
• a property where coffee, rubber, grapes, or other crops are cultivated. 
• British a housing or commercial development.
2 all the money and property owned by a particular person, especially at death: in his will, he divided his estate between his wife and daughter.
3 (also estate of the realm) a class or order regarded as forming part of the body politic, in particular (in Britain), one of the three groups constituting Parliament, now the Lords Spiritual (the heads of the Church), the Lords Temporal (the peerage), and the Commons. They are also known as the three estates. 
• dated a particular class or category of people in society: the spiritual welfare of all estates of men.
4 archaic or literary a particular state, period, or condition in life: programs for the improvement of man's estate 
the holy estate of matrimony. ORIGIN Middle English (in the sense ‘state or condition’): from Old French estat, from Latin status ‘state, condition,’ from stare ‘to stand.’
